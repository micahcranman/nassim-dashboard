# Sanitized Analyst Trust Profiles

*For the point-in-time report writer. These describe what each analyst is generally good and bad at, and how much to trust them by call type. They contain NO specific past calls, NO dates, NO outcomes — only character. A report writer uses these to judge trust without any knowledge of how a particular call turned out.*

**A note on the two sides:** a TOP call = a warning of downside / a reason to get defensive or hedge. A BOTTOM call = a reason to buy weakness. Most of these analysts are good at one side and weak at the other.

**Important limitation (read this):** these profiles are scored over each analyst's full available record, which spans only a few cycles — and for several voices only since ~2023. So (a) the confidence rests on small samples — treat each as a *prior*, not a certainty; and (b) strictly, using a profile in a point-in-time report for an early date carries mild forward-looking bias (it "knows" how later calls turned out). A fully rigorous backtest would freeze each profile to only what was knowable as of the report's date. Until then, read these as "a reasonable sense of each voice's style," not a precise, time-pure skill grade.

---

**James Check** — on-chain analyst.
Our richest on-chain commentary, but his top-calling record is THIN and MIXED — across the cycles we can see, he badly missed one major top (stayed bullish into it) and nailed another, so treat "good at tops" as a weak prior, not a settled fact. He is still our most useful on-chain regime read on the risk-OFF side: lean on his high-conviction defensive warnings, but size the confidence to a small sample. Unreliable at bottoms — roughly a coin flip, prone to turning fearful right at the lows — so don't lean on his buy calls. His value is the regime read, not price levels.

**Lyn Alden** — macro / liquidity.
The best all-rounder of the group. Strong and early at calling tops and getting defensive ahead of them. Decent at bottoms, but only when a genuine liquidity shift is behind the call — otherwise she tends to turn positive too early into a still-falling market. Most trustworthy when she genuinely changes her stance; discount her when she's just restating a long-standing bullish view. Not a source for specific price levels.

**Michael Howell** — global liquidity.
Reads the liquidity tide well — good at the DIRECTION of the next big move, weak on TIMING (often months early or late). Better at flagging tops than bottoms; his buy-the-dip calls tend to be early. Use him for which way the wind is blowing, not for when to act. Weight his genuine high-conviction shifts; ignore routine restatements.

**Macro Ops** — tactical macro.
A good fire alarm, a poor all-clear. Their "get defensive" calls are real but usually arrive late, confirming a downturn already underway. Their "buy the dip" calls are unreliable to actively wrong — fade them. Trust them most when they newly turn cautious off a named indicator flipping; ignore repeated bullish "buy the dip" refrains.

**Willy Woo** — on-chain.
A structural bull by default, so his everyday optimism carries little information. The signal is the rare moment he turns against himself — a genuine bearish flip, or taking personal defensive action — which is worth a lot as a top warning. His bottom calls are unreliable. When he's just restating optimism near a stretched market, treat it as complacency, not insight. Ignore his price targets entirely.

**The Bitcoin Layer** — macro / liquidity (a house voice, several authors).
The one genuine bottom-caller of the group — strong at identifying buyable lows, especially around Federal Reserve and liquidity pivots. But this edge only holds while the broader trend is still up; in a confirmed downtrend their bottom calls fail, so discount them then. Their top calls are mostly noise — frequent false "be careful up here" warnings that often reverse within days, so ignore those. Weight their structural bottom / liquidity-turn calls in an uptrend; ignore the rest.
